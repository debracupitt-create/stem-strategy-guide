STEM Engagement Strategy Guide

A practical tool to connect your STEM education activities to strategic priorities, select the right metrics, and build the case for sustained engagement.

For industry professionals at any stage, from first-time participants to established programs.

Step 1: Why is your organisation doing this?
Select the strategic drivers that describe why your organisation engages, or wants to engage, in STEM education. These shape the metrics and planning in later steps. Select all that apply.

Talent pipeline and recruitment

Attracting future employees, building awareness of career opportunities, strengthening graduate and apprentice pipelines.

Social licence and brand

Strengthening community presence, demonstrating public good, building organisational reputation.

Staff development and engagement

Providing leadership opportunities, improving communication skills, boosting employee satisfaction and retention.

Corporate responsibility and mandate

Meeting organisational KPIs, fulfilling public trust obligations, responding to government or board expectations.

Innovation and R&D

Accessing fresh thinking, connecting with university research, using student projects to explore early-stage ideas.

Equity and inclusion

Improving diversity in your workforce pipeline, supporting underrepresented groups, reaching underserved schools and communities.

Tips for different contexts▼
SMEs and startups

Your driver might be as straightforward as "we struggle to find qualified people." That counts as a talent pipeline driver. You do not need a formal corporate social responsibility program to have a clear reason for engaging in STEM education.
Regional and remote organisations

If your organisation is based outside a capital city, your drivers may be shaped by what is available locally. Where there are few schools or universities nearby, community presence and equity drivers may be especially relevant. Your engagement carries more weight because there is less of it in the area.
Organisations ready to formalise

If your organisation already does some STEM engagement and you want to move it from informal activity to a supported program, consider whether it can be included in your business unit plan. Linking STEM outreach to existing reporting areas (for example, workforce development, diversity targets, or community investment) makes it easier to sustain when budgets are reviewed.

Tips for different contexts▼
SMEs and startups

Your list might be short. Perhaps one internship arrangement or an occasional school visit. That is a fine starting point. Even a single activity, once written down and connected to a driver, becomes the foundation for a strategy rather than an ad hoc effort.
Regional and remote organisations

If you are based in a regional area, you may have fewer activities to list because there are fewer coordinated opportunities nearby and the logistics of participating can be significant. A shorter list is not a problem. It simply means the activities you do have are worth planning around carefully.
Organisations ready to formalise

If there are multiple people or teams across your organisation doing STEM-related activities independently (for example, one engineer doing school visits while HR runs an internship program), this stocktake can be the start of mapping what is happening across the business. Consider sharing it with colleagues to get a more complete picture.

Tips for different contexts▼
SMEs and startups

Start with two low-effort metrics. For example, "number of students engaged" and "volunteer feedback survey" are simple to collect and enough to demonstrate that you are paying attention to outcomes. You can add more ambitious metrics once your program is established.
Organisations ready to formalise

If your organisation already uses dashboards or reporting tools for executive decisions (for example, Power BI or similar), consider formatting your STEM tracking data to fit those tools. If your organisation runs multiple STEM initiatives across different teams, aggregating data into one view can show collective impact that individual activities cannot demonstrate on their own.
Step 4: Plan your next 12 months
A realistic plan with two or three activities you will actually do is more valuable than an ambitious plan that stalls. You can always add more later.

The metrics shown under each activity are the ones you selected in Step 3 that match the drivers you connected to that activity. You can remove metrics that are not relevant to a specific activity, or add additional ones using the dropdown.

Tips for different contexts▼
SMEs and startups

One well-executed activity per year is a strong start. For a small organisation where the founder or a senior team member is the person doing the engagement, time is the scarcest resource. A single school visit with proper follow-up tracking is more sustainable than committing to a program you cannot maintain.
Regional and remote organisations

If travel to schools takes significant time, consider whether some engagements could be done virtually. A recorded video presentation or an online mentoring session can extend your reach without requiring a full day out of the office for travel. This is particularly relevant if you are the only person in your organisation doing this work.
Organisations ready to formalise

If you know that colleagues in other parts of your organisation are also engaging with schools (for example, a different office or a different team), this plan is an opportunity to coordinate. A shared calendar or spreadsheet listing all STEM activities across the business, who is leading each one, and when they happen can help avoid duplication and identify gaps in geographic or sector coverage.
